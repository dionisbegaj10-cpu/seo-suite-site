What Is EEAT?

EEAT is the framework Google's quality raters use to evaluate content, especially in topics where inaccurate information could genuinely cause harm. Experience covers whether content reflects genuine firsthand use of a topic, not just research, expertise is whether the creator actually demonstrates real knowledge, and both show up through specific, concrete details that are hard to fake.

Authoritativeness is about reputation, whether a site or author is recognized by others as a legitimate source, built cumulatively through backlinks and mentions from other respected sources over time, and trustworthiness, often described as the most important of the four, covers accurate sourcing, transparency, and secure infrastructure, since without it the other three do not matter much.

EEAT is weighted especially heavily for medical, financial, legal, and safety-related content, where the cost of ranking inaccurate information is much higher than elsewhere. It is not a checklist to game, content that is genuinely accurate, written by someone who actually knows the subject, and backed by real reputation tends to naturally satisfy EEAT rather than needing to be artificially engineered to look that way.

In practice, experience often shows up as small, specific details that are genuinely hard to fabricate: a photo of the actual product being used rather than a stock image, a specific number from a real project instead of a rounded, generic claim, or a mention of a mistake made and corrected along the way. Google's own quality rater guidelines specifically call out this kind of concrete, first-hand detail as the clearest signal that separates real experience from content written about a topic secondhand.

None of the four pillars work in isolation either, a site can have genuine expertise but weak trustworthiness signals, like no clear author information or contact details, and still underperform despite the underlying quality. That's why EEAT is best understood as four interconnected signals working together, not four separate boxes to check off independently.
