Landing Pages for Dummies

A homepage tries to serve everyone, new visitors, returning customers, job seekers, press, all at once, with a dozen competing links. A landing page exists for exactly one purpose, get a specific visitor from a specific ad or email to take one specific action, and every homepage-style distraction working against that single goal should be removed.

The elements that actually move conversion rate are consistent across industries: a headline that matches exactly what the ad promised, since a mismatch makes visitors bounce within seconds convinced they clicked the wrong link, a single clear call to action repeated two or three times down the page rather than five different competing buttons, and specific proof like a real testimonial with a name, or a number like "1,200 businesses served," doing far more work than a generic "trusted by many" claim.

Removing the main site navigation from a landing page routinely increases conversions, since every additional way to leave for something else is exactly what a landing page needs to eliminate, one job for one visitor for one goal. Testing two versions of the same page against each other with real traffic beats guessing every time, moving a call-to-action button above the fold, or changing one word in a headline, can shift conversion rates by 20% or more, and there is rarely a way to know which version wins without simply testing it.

Load speed matters even more on a landing page than elsewhere on a site, since visitors arriving from a paid ad already paid for with real budget have the least patience of anyone on the site: every extra second before the page becomes usable increases the chance that click, which cost real money, gets wasted on a visitor who leaves before ever seeing the offer.

Matching the landing page's tone and imagery to the specific ad that drove the click, rather than sending every campaign to the same generic page, keeps the experience coherent and measurably improves conversion.
