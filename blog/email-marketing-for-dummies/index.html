Email Marketing for Dummies

A visitor who leaves a website without buying is not necessarily a lost sale, if their email address was captured first. Email marketing is the channel with the highest average return of any digital marketing method, and unlike social media, a business fully owns its email list, no algorithm change can suddenly hide it from subscribers overnight.

The list has to be built honestly first, through a genuine incentive like a discount code or a useful guide offered in exchange for an email address, never a purchased list, which gets flagged as spam and can get an account suspended entirely. From there, three campaign types cover most of what a small business needs, a welcome sequence for new subscribers, regular value-driven newsletters, and abandoned-cart or re-engagement emails triggered by specific customer actions.

Subject lines decide almost everything, a strong subject line can double open rates on the exact same email, and specificity beats cleverness nearly every time, "20% off through Sunday" consistently outperforms a vague, punny alternative. Testing two subject lines against each other on a small portion of the list before sending the winner to everyone else is a five-minute habit that compounds significantly over a year of sending.

Segmentation is the difference between a mediocre list and a genuinely valuable one: sending the same email to a first-time visitor and a five-year repeat customer wastes the opportunity to say something relevant to either. Even basic segmentation, separating new subscribers from existing customers, or splitting by what someone actually browsed, noticeably improves open and click rates compared to blasting one identical message to an entire list.

Cleaning the list periodically, removing subscribers who haven't opened anything in six months or more, actually improves deliverability, since email providers weigh engagement rates when deciding whether messages land in the inbox or spam.
