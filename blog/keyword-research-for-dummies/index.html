Keyword Research for Dummies

A bakery owner guessing that people search "artisan sourdough bread" might be wrong, the real number could be 40 searches a month, while "bread delivery near me" gets 2,400. Keyword research replaces guessing with data, free tools like Google Keyword Planner or Ubersuggest show exactly how many people search a phrase each month and how hard it is to rank for.

Three numbers matter more than the rest. Search volume shows how many people type that phrase monthly. Keyword difficulty (usually scored 0-100) shows how hard competing sites make it to rank. Intent shows what the searcher actually wants, someone typing "best coffee makers" is comparing options, while "coffee maker not working" wants a repair guide, not a sales pitch. Targeting the wrong intent is the single most common beginner mistake, ranking for a phrase that brings visitors who were never going to buy.

The highest-value keywords for a small business are rarely the obvious ones. "Web design" gets searched constantly and is dominated by huge agencies with years of authority, but "web design for therapists" gets far fewer searches and almost no competition, and it is easier to actually rank for and more likely to convert. Start with 10-15 specific, lower-competition phrases that match exactly what your business does, not the broad category it belongs to.

Checking what competitors already rank for is another shortcut worth using: free and low-cost tools can show which keywords are already sending traffic to a competitor's site, revealing gaps worth targeting or confirming that a term genuinely converts before investing time writing content around it. This is often faster than researching from a blank page, since it starts from proof a keyword already works for someone in the same space.

Revisiting keyword research periodically matters too, search behavior shifts over time, and a term that barely got searched a year ago can become genuinely valuable as customer language and trends evolve.

Question-based keywords deserve specific attention beyond standard phrase research, since they map directly to how people phrase voice searches and AI assistant queries alike. Tools that surface actual questions people ask ('People Also Ask' boxes, autocomplete suggestions, community forums like Reddit or Quora) often reveal genuinely valuable, lower-competition opportunities that standard keyword-volume tools miss entirely, since a full question rarely shows up as a distinct trackable phrase in traditional keyword databases.

Grouping related keywords into topic clusters, rather than treating each one as an isolated target, tends to produce stronger results than optimizing single pages for single keywords in isolation. A cluster of interconnected content covering a topic from multiple angles, linked together internally, signals topical depth to search engines in a way that scattered, disconnected pages targeting the same broad subject cannot replicate.

Frequently asked questions

Should I always target the highest-search-volume keywords?

Not necessarily — high-volume keywords are usually more competitive and less specific. Lower-volume, more specific keywords ('long-tail') often convert better because they match clearer intent.

How many keywords should one page target?

Usually one primary keyword plus a handful of closely related terms — spreading a single page across too many unrelated keywords tends to dilute its relevance for all of them.
