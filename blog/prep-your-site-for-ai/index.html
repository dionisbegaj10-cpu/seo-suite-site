Prep Your Site for AI

The first era of the internet was about simply having a website, the second was about ranking on Google, and the next is about being understood by AI. Businesses now need to prepare for a world where customers ask AI systems for answers and recommendations instead of typing a search query and clicking through results themselves. The first step is creating clear business information, since AI needs clarity above almost everything else, meaning a website should plainly explain what a company does, who it helps, its services, its expertise, and its location without vague, generic language.

The most reliable way to teach AI about a business's expertise is to teach humans first, building content around the actual questions customers ask, whether that is what SEO is, how much a website costs, how branding works, or what AI visibility means. Every one of these becomes a signal AI can learn from. Building authority is equally important, since AI needs trust signals like quality content, reviews, partnerships, industry mentions, and case studies to form a picture of a business worth recommending.

Improving website experience matters too, since a modern website needs to be fast, secure, mobile friendly, and easy to navigate, while a poor experience creates poor signals for visitors, Google, and AI systems evaluating the site alike. A useful habit is thinking like the customer, asking what questions someone would actually have before choosing a company, and then answering those questions directly on the site, which helps three audiences at once: customers, Google, and AI assistants.

AI search is likely to keep getting more personalized, with people asking for recommendations tailored to their specific situation rather than just searching for products. Businesses that have already built real expertise and trust will have the advantage when that shift fully arrives. Preparing for AI search is not about chasing the newest technology trend, it is about becoming easier to understand, and a business that clearly explains who it is, what it does, and why it matters has a real shot at being discovered everywhere, not just on a search results page.

Preparing a site to be readable and citable by AI systems starts with the same technical basics traditional SEO always required — a crawlable site structure, fast load times, and unblocked access for crawlers — since AI systems can't cite a page they can't access any more than a traditional search engine could rank one. Checking robots.txt to confirm AI crawlers (GPTBot, ClaudeBot, PerplexityBot, Google-Extended) aren't blocked is a specific, easy first check many sites skip.

Beyond access, structure matters more for AI extraction than it does for a human skimming a page: clear headings that match how people actually phrase questions, direct answers stated early rather than buried after several paragraphs of introduction, and structured data (schema markup) that gives an AI system unambiguous, machine-readable context about the page's content all increase the odds a system can confidently extract and cite specific information rather than skipping the page for a clearer source.

Frequently asked questions

What's the single most important thing to fix first for AI visibility?

Making sure AI crawlers aren't blocked in robots.txt — it's a simple technical check, but if bots like GPTBot or PerplexityBot are disallowed, none of your other AI-optimization work matters.

Does preparing a site for AI search hurt traditional SEO?

No — the two overlap significantly. Clear structure, authoritative content, and proper schema markup help both traditional rankings and AI citation at the same time.
